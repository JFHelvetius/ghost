\begin{abstract}
\textbf{Thesis: safety claims should be executable and falsifiable
through a reproducible citation.} We introduce \emph{executable
safety citations}: a reproducibility pattern that lets a third
party verify a safety claim against a content-addressed autonomy
log using a single command. The pattern composes seven existing
ingredients ---
ADRs, content-addressed MCAP telemetry, pure-function verifiers,
Hypothesis property tests, CI gating, tagged releases, and
OIDC-signed PyPI wheels --- into one shippable unit, with
underlying invariants additionally checked by TLA+/TLC. We
instantiate the pattern on a five-property reference autonomy
supervisor (BAUD/ERUR/MD/RLB/FPB), verify it under three
calibration policies, exercise it under six categories of
injected bugs (all detected), and run the verifier unchanged on
a real PX4 v1.10 flight log --- on which the verifier
\emph{discriminates}: two independently buggy components, swapped
into the same physical flight, each flip BAUD-v1 from HOLDS to
VIOLATED, while the four other properties remain HOLD. The
pattern, not the individual properties, is the contribution. The
artifact is re-runnable from
\texttt{pip install project-ghost==0.2.3}.
\end{abstract}

\noindent\textbf{Keywords:} safety citation patterns, reproducible
safety verification, runtime verification, content-addressed
telemetry, TLA+, MCAP.

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

Safety claims in autonomy research are typically asserted in prose
and illustrated by simulation videos that the reader cannot re-run.
The literature on uncertainty in autonomy is rich --- Bayesian
filters, calibration of probabilistic predictions, fault detection
and isolation, runtime supervisors --- but the gap between
\emph{the theory exists} and \emph{this specific run, on this
specific code, satisfies the property} is rarely closed
operationally. A third party cannot, in practice, verify another
team's safety claim against a recorded run.

We describe Project Ghost, an open-source platform whose primary
contribution is a citation pattern that closes that gap: a third
party can verify any cited safety claim against the recorded run by
running \texttt{ghost verify-properties --mcap <log>} from a wheel
installed via \texttt{pip}. Two of the properties (BAUD-v1 and
ERUR-v1) plus the recovery latency bound and the FPB counter
automaton are mechanically verified by TLA+/TLC over the abstract
state space of the reference policy pair. The whole pipeline runs
end-to-end on a real PX4 flight log in CI.

\subsection{The single claim}

\textbf{A safety claim about an autonomy system can today be
asserted by its authors and illustrated in their venues, but it
cannot be falsified by a third party who was not in the room when
the run happened.} Project Ghost closes that gap. A safety claim
ships as a content-addressed autonomy log plus a single shell
command (\texttt{ghost verify-properties --mcap <log>}); anyone
with the wheel and the log reproduces the verdict --- or
contradicts it. We call this pattern an \textbf{executable safety
citation}. The body of the paper is the operational evidence that
the pattern works.

\section{Background and related work}
\label{sec:related}

\textbf{Underlying ingredients.} Bayesian and particle filtering
[Thrun et al.\ 2005], calibration of probabilistic predictions
[Vovk et al.\ 2005], runtime verification [Bartocci et al.\ 2018],
TLA+/TLC [Lamport 2002], MCAP [Foxglove 2022].

\textbf{Closest tooling prior work.} RTAMT [Niković et al.\ 2020]:
STL monitors over CPS logs; STL formula language, no mechanically
verified proof layer. MoonLight [Bartocci et al.\ 2020]: STREL
monitor; spatial focus. ROSMonitoring [Ferrando et al.\ 2020] and
ROSRV [Huang et al.\ 2014]: live ROS-middleware monitors, online.
Safe RL via shielding [Jansen et al.\ 2020]: online enforcement.
Conformal prediction for robot safety [Chakraborty et al.\ 2024]:
predictive. Supervisory control of timed automata
[Flordal et al.\ 2022]: synthesises new supervisors. Surveys
[Rizaldi et al.\ 2020] note absence of mechanically verified TLA+
specs for autonomy supervisors specifically.

\textbf{Industrial practice.} Waymo, NASA NFM, PX4
\texttt{commander}, Autoware, Cruise produce safety artefacts at
scales Ghost does not. They share organisational properties Ghost
does not: teams of safety engineers and proprietary access to
telemetry, testing infrastructure, and regulators. The niche we
believe Ghost fills, complementary to those efforts, is the gap
between ``this software is safe'' (a closed claim signed by an
organisation) and ``here is the verifier and the log; check it
yourself'' (an open claim citable by a third party). The pattern
is a primitive those cases could cite, not a substitute for them.

\section{The property set and the verifier}
\label{sec:properties}

We instantiate the pattern on five properties of a reference
closed-loop autonomy supervisor:

\begin{itemize}[noitemsep,topsep=2pt]
  \item \textbf{BAUD-v1} (ADR-0031): when drift is detected the
        agent emits no non-conservative action.
  \item \textbf{ERUR-v1} (ADR-0032): when drift is absent and
        belief is KNOWN the agent emits PROCEED.
  \item \textbf{MD-v1} (ADR-0033): the calibrator never inflates
        confidence ($\mathrm{adjusted} \preceq \mathrm{raw}$).
  \item \textbf{RLB-v1} (ADR-0034): structural recovery latency
        bound (\S\ref{sec:mechanical}).
  \item \textbf{FPB-v1} (ADR-0035): empirical fire-rate observer.
\end{itemize}

Each property is stated as a binding ADR, verified by a pure
function over the MCAP, exercised by Hypothesis property tests,
witnessed inline in every reference smoke, and self-enforced on
every push by CI.

\textbf{CLI surface.} \texttt{pip install project-ghost==0.2.3}
followed by \texttt{ghost verify-properties --mcap <log>} yields
exit code \texttt{0} iff every property holds, with a deterministic
JSON report. The verifier is a pure function over the
content-addressed MCAP; no replay, no simulation, no trust in the
producer.

\section{Mechanical verification (compact)}
\label{sec:mechanical}

Three TLA+ specifications jointly cover the five properties with 11
invariants verified by TLC over the bounded state space, in CI on
every push.

\texttt{BaudErur.tla} ($M{=}2, K{=}1, W{=}3$) verifies BAUD-v1,
ERUR-v1, the partition theorem $\mathrm{BAUD} \oplus \mathrm{ERUR}$,
MD-v1, and the structural history bound. \texttt{Rlb.tla} ($W{=}4$)
mirrors the verifier algorithm under the consecutive-drift
hypothesis and mechanises the recovery latency bound below.
\texttt{Fpb.tla} ($\mathrm{MAX\_CYCLES}{=}8$) checks the FPB
counter automaton's structural well-formedness.

\paragraph{Recovery latency bound (RLB-v1, transient regime).}
Let the outcome stream contain a transient drift of $N \le W$
consecutive dirty outcomes followed by clean outcomes. Let
$\mathrm{peak} = N$ and $L$ be the dirty-run length. Then
$L = \mathrm{peak} + W - 1$, attained with equality
(proof in \S\ref{app:theorem}).

\subsection{Discrimination, not just all-HOLDS}
\label{sec:realflight-discrim}

A naive run produces all-HOLDS on the real ULog (MCAP SHA-256
\texttt{49fd0a48\ldots}). That row alone does not establish that
the verifier \emph{catches} anything on real telemetry --- a
vacuous verifier would produce the same row. The load-bearing
experiment is the verdict \emph{delta} on the same physical
flight.

\section{Limitations}
\label{sec:limits}

\begin{itemize}[noitemsep,topsep=2pt]
  \item \textbf{Sim, not hardware.} Non-vacuous safety verdicts
        require an independent ground-truth source. Candidate
        ADR-0037.
  \item \textbf{Reference policies only.} TLA+ specs target the
        reference. Non-reference policies need their own.
  \item \textbf{Bounded TLC.} Behaviour at production-scale
        constants relies on property tests; TLA+ fills the
        small-but-exhaustive corner.
  \item \textbf{Python$\leftrightarrow$TLA+ bridge by inspection.}
        Automating it is future work.
\end{itemize}

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

\textbf{Ghost packages a safety claim, its run, and its verifier
as a reproducible unit a third party can re-derive.} The
load-bearing contribution is operational: an ADR, an MCAP, a
pure-function CLI verifier, property tests, mechanical checks
(TLA+/TLC) and an OIDC-signed PyPI wheel --- assembled so that
the citation \emph{is} the falsification mechanism. The individual
ingredients are standard; the assembly, in our surveyed scope, is
missing. The artifact is re-runnable from
\texttt{pip install project-ghost==0.2.3}.

\section{Proof of the recovery latency bound}
\label{app:theorem}

Trace the window state cycle by cycle, noting the sliding-window
invariant: at cycle $t$, the window contains the last
$\min(t, W)$ outcomes. Accumulation phase (cycles $1..N$): each
cycle adds one dirty outcome; the window is not yet full
($N \le W$); dirty count rises from $1$ to $N = \mathrm{peak}$.
Saturation phase (cycles $N+1..W$): clean outcomes arrive,
window not yet full, count stays at $\mathrm{peak}$. Flush phase
(cycles $W+1..W+\mathrm{peak}-1$): each clean expels the oldest
(dirty) entry; count drops by 1 per cycle from $\mathrm{peak}$ to
$1$. Recovery at cycle $W+\mathrm{peak}$: last dirty expelled,
count $0$. Total dirty cycles: $N + (W - N) + (\mathrm{peak} - 1)
= W + \mathrm{peak} - 1$. Since $\mathrm{peak} = N$ in the
transient regime, $L = \mathrm{peak} + W - 1$. \qed
